## Federal baseline refresh

**P.L. 119-37**, signed by President Trump on November 12, 2025, fundamentally redefines hemp under 7 U.S.C. §1639o effective **November 12, 2026**. Section 781 of Division B replaces the delta-9-only threshold with a **total THC standard** (inclusive of THCa, delta-8, and all THC forms) at ≤0.3% dry weight, imposes a **0.4 mg total THC per container** ceiling for finished hemp-derived cannabinoid products, and expressly prohibits synthesized or non-naturally-occurring cannabinoids (delta-8 via CBD isomerization, HHC, THC-O). CRS estimates approximately **95% of current hemp-derived cannabinoid products** would become federally unlawful. Legislative responses are pending: H.R. 6209 (repeal), H.R. 7024/S. 3686 (delay to Nov. 2028), and the Cannabinoid Safety and Regulation Act.

On **July 29, 2025**, the FDA recommended **Schedule I classification** for concentrated/synthetic 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH), specifically targeting isolated 7-OH products — not natural kratom leaf — citing potent opioid receptor binding exceeding morphine, proliferation in gas stations and vape shops, and rising poison control reports. FDA Commissioner Makary and HHS Secretary RFK Jr. simultaneously issued warning letters to seven companies. The **DEA has not yet taken final scheduling action** as of April 2026. On March 27, 2026, Rep. Rob Bresnahan (R-PA) urged the DEA to pursue emergency scheduling. The FY2026 appropriations conference report includes language supporting "swift administrative action." Legal analysts note the evidence base for emergency scheduling may be thin (53 poison control calls over 3 months, no confirmed 7-OH-alone fatalities).

## ⚠️ High-risk states for hemp/kratom/psychedelic operators (April 2026)

**Idaho** remains the single highest-risk state in this batch and arguably the nation. THCa, delta-8, and delta-9 hemp products are **all banned** under the zero-THC finished product standard. Psilocybin spores are uniquely illegal. Four competing kratom bills in the 2026 session could either regulate or outright ban kratom, creating existential regulatory uncertainty. **Louisiana** has enacted an overlapping set of restrictions that effectively lock out most product categories. THCa is functionally banned via the total THC redefinition (Act 752); kratom and 7-OH are Schedule I (SB 154/Act 41, effective August 1, 2025); hemp products face **5 mg/serving, 40 mg/package** caps with ATC licensing; flower and vape products are prohibited; gas station sales banned. Four of seven tracked compounds are banned or effectively banned.

**Indiana** bans kratom, 7-OH, and likely MGM-15 under its 2014 synthetic drug statute, while psilocybin is Schedule I. SB 478 legalized regulated hemp sales, but the AG's continuing opposition creates enforcement risk. Three to four compounds are outright banned.

**Kansas** joins this list effective **July 1, 2026**, when HB 2365 adds 7-OH (and likely all kratom) plus HHC to Schedule I. Combined with ambiguous hemp enforcement and psilocybin prohibition, Kansas is transitioning from moderate to high-risk.

**Kentucky** warrants a watch listing: despite having robust hemp and kratom frameworks, **HB 757 threatens to repeal the KCPA** and ban all kratom, and concentrated 7-OH is already classified Schedule I via executive action. If HB 757 is signed before the April 15 session end, Kentucky would shift to high-risk for kratom/7-OH operators.

States with **more than 3 of 7 compounds banned or heavily restricted**: Idaho (4+), Louisiana (4+), Indiana (3–4), Kansas (3+ effective July 2026).
